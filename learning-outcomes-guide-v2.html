Foundations · 01

What Is a Learning Outcome?

A learning outcome is a promise about observable student performance — not a description of what will be covered, and not a hope about how students will feel. It governs everything that follows: instruction, assessment, feedback, and grading.

The most important distinction in outcome writing is between a coverage statement and a performance statement. A coverage statement describes what an instructor will do: "This course will introduce students to Pauline theology." A performance statement describes what a student will be able to do: "Students will analyze the logical structure of a Pauline argument and identify its theological claims."

This distinction matters because only performance statements can be assessed. You cannot write a rubric for "introduction to." You cannot give feedback on "exposure to." But you can observe, grade, and give precise feedback on "analyze the logical structure" — because that is a cognitive act that produces something visible.

The test of any outcome: can you write a rubric for it? If not, it is not yet an outcome.

Ralph Tyler established the foundational principle in 1949: objectives must be stated in terms of student behavior, not teacher behavior. Mager (1962) specified the structure of a well-formed objective. Bloom et al. (1956) gave the principle a classificatory vocabulary, revised and refined by Anderson and Krathwohl in 2001. Biggs (1999) showed why consistency between outcomes, instruction, and assessment is the structural integrity of any course.

The three properties of a well-formed outcome

Observable: another person can see the student performing it.
Measurable: a rubric can specify what better and worse performance looks like.
Honest: the cognitive demand stated in the verb matches what instruction and assessment actually require.

Foundations · 02

Foundational Literature

These are the primary intellectual sources for outcome writing, alignment, and curriculum design. Understanding the argument each scholar makes — not just their name — is what allows their work to be applied rather than merely cited.

Tyler (1949) — The four questions

Ralph Tyler's Basic Principles of Curriculum and Instruction is the foundational text of outcomes-based curriculum development. Tyler posed four questions every curriculum designer must answer: What purposes should the program attain? What experiences can be provided to attain them? How can those experiences be organized? And how do we determine whether purposes are being attained? These four questions map directly onto the modern PLO → CLO → ALO pipeline. Tyler's insistence that objectives precede and govern curriculum design — not vice versa — remains the bedrock of backward design.

Bloom et al. (1956) — The original taxonomy

Following educator conferences between 1949 and 1953, Bloom and collaborators published a hierarchical classification of cognitive educational goals: Knowledge, Comprehension, Application, Analysis, Synthesis, and Evaluation. The aim was a common language for distinguishing lower-order from higher-order thinking. Krathwohl et al. (1964) extended the framework to the affective domain (emotions, attitudes, values); Simpson (1972) addressed the psychomotor domain. The 2001 Anderson and Krathwohl revision is the version used in all contemporary instructional design and accreditation contexts.

Mager (1962) — How to write objectives

Robert Mager's Preparing Instructional Objectives established not how to classify objectives but how to write them. Mager's core principle: a well-formed objective must describe observable, measurable learner performance — not content coverage or teacher activity. He introduced the three-part structure (performance, conditions, criteria) that became the basis of the ABCD model. His verdict on vague verbs — "know," "understand," "appreciate" — was unambiguous: they are inadmissible because they cannot be observed or measured.

Anderson & Krathwohl (2001) — The revision

Lorin Anderson and David Krathwohl revised Bloom's taxonomy forty-five years after its original publication. Two pivotal changes: nouns became verbs (the six levels were renamed as cognitive processes to emphasize active performance: Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create — with Synthesis and Evaluation reordered, placing Create at the apex); and a Knowledge Dimension was added, crossing cognitive process with knowledge type. See the Knowledge Dimension section for full detail.

Biggs (1999, 2011) — Constructive alignment

John Biggs introduced the principle that unifies all three levels of outcomes. The term has two components: constructive — learning is built by what students do, not what teachers do; the verb embedded in each outcome signals the cognitive work students must perform. And alignment — course outcomes, teaching and learning activities, and assessment tasks must all reinforce the same goals. Biggs's core formulation: if an outcome says analyze, the learning activities must require analysis, and the assessment must measure analysis.

Wiggins & McTighe (1998, 2005) — Backward design

Understanding by Design operationalized Tyler's questions into a three-stage process: identify desired results (Stage 1), determine acceptable evidence (Stage 2), plan learning experiences and instruction (Stage 3). The key insight: "Our lessons, units, and courses should be logically inferred from the results sought, not derived from the methods, books, and activities with which we are most comfortable." Design begins at the end and works backward to instruction.

Fink (2003) — Significant learning

L. Dee Fink's Creating Significant Learning Experiences grew from dissatisfaction with purely cognitive frameworks. Fink observed that many outcomes students identify as most significant — shifts in professional identity, changed values, deepened self-awareness — do not map onto Bloom's cognitive hierarchy. His six-dimension taxonomy arranges learning not as a hierarchy but as an interactive web, where growth in any one dimension tends to stimulate growth in the others. See the Fink section for full treatment.

Knowles (1968–1998) — Andragogy

Malcolm Knowles addressed the fundamental question of how adults learn — a question with direct implications for how outcomes are framed, sequenced, and communicated in post-secondary and seminary contexts. His six principles of andragogy specify the conditions under which adult learners will most readily engage with and retain learning. See the Knowles section for the full framework.

The Three Levels · 01

PLOs, CLOs, and ALOs

Learning outcomes in higher education operate at three hierarchical levels. The relationship is a cascade: PLOs are realized through CLOs, which are demonstrated through ALOs. Together they form a coherent, traceable chain of intent, instruction, and evidence.

Level 1 — Program
Program Learning Outcomes (PLOs)
What graduates of the entire program will know, be able to do, and value by the time they complete their degree. Intentionally broad — representing integrated competencies that transcend any single course. Set the Bloom's ceiling for the program.
3–7 per program Higher-order verbs (L4–L6) Flows from mission Degree-appropriate
↓
Level 2 — Course
Course Learning Outcomes (CLOs)
What students can do by the end of a specific course. More specific and immediately measurable than PLOs. Each CLO must explicitly support one or more PLOs. Bloom's level is constrained by where the course sits in the program sequence — not by ambition alone.
3–7 per course Maps to named PLO Appropriate to sequence position Observable within one course
↓
Level 3 — Assignment / Lesson
Assignment / Lesson-Level Outcomes (ALOs)
What students can do after a specific assignment, class session, module, or lesson. The most granular level. Achieving a set of ALOs is the mechanism through which a CLO is attained. Must include the full ABCD structure where precision matters.
Single skill or concept Single measurable verb At or below parent CLO level Directly assessable in the task
ALO example — three ALOs building toward one CLO

Parent CLO (Analyze, L4): Analyze the logical structure of a Pauline argument, identifying its theological claims and the evidence marshaled for each.

ALO 1 (Remember, L1): Identify the major argumentative units in Galatians 3 given a provided structural outline.
ALO 2 (Understand, L2): Describe in one paragraph the logical relationship between Galatians 3:1–14 and 3:15–29.
ALO 3 (Analyze, L4): Compare the theological claims in Galatians 3 with those in Romans 4, citing at least three formal argumentative parallels in a structured written response.

Note: All three ALOs are at or below the parent CLO's L4 ceiling. An ALO can never demand more of students than the CLO it serves.

The Three Levels · 02

The Cascade Principle

The three levels do not merely coexist — they aggregate. Reading downward, each level is achieved through the one below. Reading upward, each level provides evidence of the one above.

A PLO is demonstrated when a student achieves multiple CLOs across the program. A CLO is demonstrated when a student achieves multiple ALOs within a course. An ALO is demonstrated through successful performance on a specific assignment or activity.

Reading upward: ALOs aggregate into CLO attainment. CLOs aggregate into PLO attainment. PLO attainment is the program's evidence that its educational mission was achieved.

The cascade in practice at Westminster

PLO: Defend the system of doctrine in the Westminster Standards using biblical argumentation.

Multiple CLOs serve this PLO across different courses — a Systematic Theology CLO at the Understand level, an Apologetics CLO at the Analyze level, a Capstone CLO at the Evaluate level.

Within each course, multiple ALOs serve each CLO — lesson objectives on specific passages, discussion posts on particular doctrines, draft arguments on targeted objections.

No single ALO proves the PLO. No single CLO proves it. The aggregate of evidence across the program is what the PLO names.

The Three Levels · 03

The Verb Hierarchy Rule

The cognitive level encoded in a Bloom's verb must obey a strict rule of vertical alignment. This is not a guideline — it is the logical requirement for a coherent outcome chain.

Verb hierarchy — cognitive ceiling rule

≥
CLO verb

≥
ALO verb

Rule: The cognitive level of an ALO must be at or below its parent CLO. The cognitive level of a CLO must be at or below its parent PLO. You cannot claim a program produces graduates who evaluate if no course ever requires evaluation, and no assignment ever demands it.

This rule ensures logical coherence in both directions. Violating it upward (an ALO higher than its CLO) means the assignment is demanding cognitive work the course outcome never promised to develop. Violating it downward (a CLO far below its PLO) means the course is not contributing meaningfully to the program goal it claims to serve.

The most common violation

A CLO written at Evaluate (L5) but assessed only with a multiple-choice exam (L1). The outcome is honest in its ambition but dishonest in its assessment. The cognitive demand must be consistent from outcome → instruction → assignment → rubric criterion. Misalignment here means students are graded on something different from what was promised.

Bloom's Taxonomy · 02

The Knowledge Dimension

The 2001 Anderson and Krathwohl revision added a second dimension to Bloom's taxonomy. Outcomes now sit within a two-dimensional matrix crossing cognitive process (the six levels) with knowledge type. This is one of the most underused tools in outcome writing.

Factual
Basic facts & terminology
The discrete elements of a discipline — terms, definitions, specific details. Students must know these to engage with the subject at all.
Conceptual
Interrelationships & theories
Classifications, principles, generalizations, theories, models, and structures — how basic elements function together within a larger framework.
Procedural
Methods & techniques
How to do something — subject-specific skills, algorithms, techniques, and criteria for knowing when to use which procedure.
Metacognitive
Self-awareness of learning
Knowledge about one's own cognition and learning — strategic knowledge, self-monitoring, awareness of one's strengths and gaps as a learner.

The Knowledge Dimension matters practically because two outcomes can share the same cognitive level but require very different instruction and assessment. "Recall the definition of justification" (Remember × Factual) and "Recall which exegetical method to use for narrative versus epistle" (Remember × Procedural) are both L1 — but they are not the same kind of learning and should not be taught or assessed identically.

Using the two-dimensional matrix

When writing an outcome, ask two questions: what is the cognitive level (which of the six Bloom's processes)? And what is the knowledge type (factual, conceptual, procedural, or metacognitive)? A complete outcome specification names both. A systematic theology course outcome might target Conceptual × Evaluate. A preaching practicum outcome might target Procedural × Create. An outcomes mapping exercise across a program is more revealing when it tracks both dimensions rather than cognitive level alone.

Writing Outcomes · 01

The ABCD Formula

Mager's (1962) three-part structure — performance, conditions, criteria — developed into the widely-used ABCD model. Every well-formed outcome contains all four components, though Audience is often implicit.

Audience
Who are the learners? Usually implicit ("students will be able to…") but becomes important when the audience shapes the performance standard.
Always present
B
Behavior
What observable action will they perform? This is the Bloom's verb — the cognitive act that is visible, assessable, and honest about what is being learned.
Always present
C
Condition
Under what circumstances? "Given a case study…" "Using provided sources…" "Without reference materials…" The condition specifies the performance context.
Recommended
D
Degree
To what standard? "Citing at least 3 sources…" "With supporting rationale from learning theory…" "Identifying at least two design constraints…" Makes the bar explicit.

Writing Outcomes · 02

Before & After Examples

Each example pair shows a failing outcome and its corrected version, with a diagnosis of what failed and why the revision works. Use these as models when reviewing outcomes with faculty.

✕
"Students will understand the Westminster Confession of Faith."
Fails: "understand" without specification is unmeasurable. What level of understanding? Recall a summary? Analyze its argument structure? There is no content specificity and no condition.
✓
Summarize [L2] the major doctrines of the Westminster Confession of Faith [content] by identifying the biblical citations each chapter provides [condition], producing a one-paragraph précis per chapter [degree].
Works: "summarize" is observable and at a specified Bloom's level, the content domain is precise, the condition defines the performance context, and the degree makes the deliverable concrete.
✕
"Students will appreciate the richness of Pauline theology."
Fails on all four ABCD dimensions: "appreciate" is unmeasurable; "richness" is not a content domain; there is no condition; there is no degree. This is an aspiration, not an outcome.
✓
Analyze [L4] the logical structure of an assigned Pauline epistle [content] by identifying its major argumentative units, the theological claim of each, and the evidence marshaled in its support [condition + degree].
Works: "analyze" is an L4 Bloom's verb producing visible work; the content is genre-specific; the condition specifies the analytical task; the degree names what the analysis must produce.
✕
"Students will know how to preach."
Fails: "know" is unmeasurable; "how to preach" is too broad to assess; there is no condition specifying what counts as evidence of knowing; there is no degree. The outcome describes an instructor's hope, not a student's performance.
✓
Construct [L6] an expository sermon [content] from an assigned Old Testament narrative text, demonstrating movement from exegetical observation to theological application to congregational address [condition], presenting the sermon orally for a minimum of 20 minutes to a specified audience [degree].
Works: "construct" is a Create-level verb producing a deliverable; the content genre is specific; the condition specifies the expected movement of thought; the degree makes the performance context and duration explicit.
✕
"Students will be exposed to different models of Christian witness in church history."
Fails: "be exposed to" describes a teaching activity, not student performance. The student is entirely passive in this statement. It cannot be assessed at any level.
✓
Compare [L4] two historical models of Christian witness from different cultural contexts [content], evaluating the effectiveness of each against biblical and missional criteria [condition] in a 1,500-word analytical essay [degree].
Works: "compare" at L4 produces visible analytical work; two specific historical cases is a bounded content domain; the evaluative criteria are specified; the deliverable format and length provide a degree standard.
Alignment · 01

Constructive Alignment

Biggs's constructive alignment requires that what a program promises, what a course teaches, and what an assignment assesses all pull in the same direction. This chain is the structural integrity of the curriculum.

Level 1 — Program
Program Learning Outcome
What a graduate can do. Flows from institutional mission. Sets the Bloom's ceiling — no CLO in any course should require cognitive work the PLO does not authorize.
↓
Level 2 — Course
Course Learning Outcome
What students can do by course end. Must trace to a named PLO explicitly. Bloom's level constrained by program sequence position and PLO ceiling.
↓
Level 3 — Assignment / Lesson
Assignment / Lesson-Level Outcome
The performance task producing evidence of the CLO. Bloom's level must match the CLO verb. Rubric criteria must assess exactly what the CLO states — not general quality.

The three alignment questions

Alignment audit — three questions before finalizing any course

1. Can I trace every CLO to a named PLO? If not, the CLO is either orphaned or the PLO set has a gap.

2. Can I trace every assignment to a named CLO? Students doing work no CLO covers is a misalignment — or the CLO is incomplete.

3. Does the Bloom's level of the assessment match the CLO verb? A "defend" CLO assessed with a recall quiz is a broken chain at the most critical joint.

Alignment · 02

Curriculum Mapping

The curriculum map is the documentary evidence of PLO/CLO alignment. It is a two-dimensional matrix plotting courses against PLOs, annotated to show how each course contributes to each program goal across the full sequence.

The standard annotation system most closely aligned with Bloom's progression is the Introduce / Develop / Master (I/D/M) notation, sometimes called Introduce / Reinforce / Master. Cells are annotated to show the stage of engagement — never expecting mastery in a course that introduces, never leaving a PLO introduced but never developed. Every PLO should appear in multiple courses, and at least one course should assess it at the Master level.

Curriculum map diagnostic — what to look for

Orphaned PLOs: A PLO column that shows only I — introduced somewhere but never developed or assessed — is a structural failure. The program promised something it never taught to mastery.

Overloaded courses: A course row with M in every column is trying to do too much. Mastery-level work requires prior Introduce and Develop stages — a course cannot produce mastery of content it is also introducing.

Missing pathways: A PLO with no I stage means students arrive at Develop-level courses without the prerequisite schema. This produces cognitive overload rather than learning.

Alignment · 03

Introduce → Develop → Master

No single course should attempt to take students from L1 to L5 on the same content. The IDM framework maps which course owns which stage of development toward a PLO — and at what Bloom's level.

Introduce
First exposure
Students encounter the concept for the first time. Scaffolding is high. The cognitive goal is schema formation — building the foundation that later courses will develop. Instructor carries most of the cognitive load.
L1–L2 · Remember / Understand
Develop
Build complexity
Students apply and analyze with decreasing scaffolding. Moving toward independent use. Structured practice and feedback are essential. The schema formed in Introduce is tested and refined through application.
L3–L4 · Apply / Analyze
Master
Independent performance
Students demonstrate the skill without scaffolding, in novel or high-stakes contexts. This is where CLOs converge with PLO-level expectations. The program outcome is finally, fully demonstrated.
L5–L6 · Evaluate / Create
IDM across a program — Westminster example

PLO: Defend the system of doctrine in the Westminster Standards using biblical argumentation and historical theological sources. (Evaluate, L5)

· Year 1 — Biblical Introduction: Summarize the major doctrines of the Westminster Standards — Introduce (L2)
· Year 2 — Systematic Theology: Examine a contemporary controversy by identifying which Standards apply — Develop (L4)
· Year 3 — Apologetics Seminar: Defend a doctrinal position against a specified objection — Master (L5)

All three CLOs serve the same PLO. None tries to do everything. The Introduce stage gives students the schema; Develop gives them practice with decreasing support; Master demands independent performance.

Complementary Frameworks · 01

Fink's Taxonomy of Significant Learning

L. Dee Fink (2003) developed a complementary taxonomy for learning outcomes that Bloom's cognitive hierarchy does not fully address. Use Bloom's as the precision instrument for cognitive demand; use Fink's as the audit framework for the full scope of intended learning.

Dimension 1
Foundational Knowledge
Remembering and understanding key information, facts, concepts, and theories — the bedrock that enables all further learning. Most closely aligns with Bloom's L1–L2.
explain · define · describe · identify
Dimension 2
Application
Using knowledge in practical ways — critical thinking, creative problem-solving, managing projects, psychomotor skills. Broader than Bloom's Apply: spans L3–L6 and includes skills Bloom's cognitive taxonomy does not address.
apply · design · solve · demonstrate
Dimension 3
Integration
Making connections across ideas, disciplines, courses, and lived experience. Aligns with Bloom's L4–L6 but adds a cross-domain relational quality that Bloom's intra-disciplinary model does not capture.
connect · synthesize · relate · compare across
Dimension 4
Human Dimension
Learning about oneself and others — how this knowledge changes self-understanding, interpersonal effectiveness, or empathy. Has no direct equivalent in Bloom's cognitive taxonomy; aligns with the affective domain.
reflect on · describe one's own · collaborate
Dimension 5
Caring
Developing new or deepened interests, values, dispositions, and emotional commitments to the subject or profession. Aligns with Bloom's affective domain. Knowles' andragogy predicts adult learners will find this dimension particularly motivating.
value · commit to · develop a stance toward
Dimension 6
Learning How to Learn
Becoming more intentional, metacognitive, and self-directed as a learner. Maps to Bloom's Metacognitive Knowledge Dimension. The dimension Knowles' "self-directedness" principle most directly addresses.
self-assess · monitor · inquire · set goals

How to use Bloom's and Fink's together

Write outcomes with Bloom's verbs to ensure cognitive precision and measurability. Audit outcomes with Fink's dimensions to check whether the program only addresses Foundational Knowledge and Application — leaving Integration, Human Dimension, Caring, and Learning How to Learn underserved.

The Fink audit — a diagnostic question for programs

A PLO written purely from Bloom might read: "Graduates will evaluate instructional design solutions against established principles of learning science."

Audited through Fink: this outcome addresses Foundational Knowledge and Application, but says nothing about how graduates think about their professional identity (Human Dimension), what they value in the field (Caring), or how they continue to develop after graduation (Learning How to Learn).

A program that adds PLOs targeting these dimensions produces graduates who are not just technically competent but professionally formed and committed to continued growth — which is precisely what Westminster's formational mission requires.

Complementary Frameworks · 02

Knowles' Andragogy

Malcolm Knowles addresses the fundamental question of how adults learn — with direct implications for how outcomes are framed, sequenced, and communicated at Westminster, where all students are adult learners by design.

PrincipleCore assumptionImplication for outcome design
Need to Know
Adults need to understand why they must learn something before committing effort to it.
Outcomes should be transparent and rationale-driven. Communicate the why of each outcome explicitly — how it serves their vocation.
Self-Concept
Adults see themselves as autonomous, self-directed learners and resist being told what to do.
Outcomes should invite agency where possible — offering choice in demonstration formats or application contexts.
Role of Experience
Adults bring rich prior experience that serves as both a resource and a potential filter for new learning.
Outcomes should build on and connect to prior knowledge. Seminary students often have pastoral, vocational, and theological experience that should be activated.
Readiness to Learn
Adults' readiness is triggered by real-life tasks and role demands, not arbitrary academic sequences.
Outcomes should be positioned within authentic, professionally relevant contexts — ministry situations, pastoral problems, congregational realities.
Problem-Centered
Adults are problem-centered rather than subject-centered; they seek immediate applicability.
Outcomes should emphasize practical application, not abstract content mastery. Frame outcomes around problems students will actually face.
Motivation to Learn
Adults are driven more by intrinsic motivators (growth, calling, professional identity) than external rewards.
Outcomes should reflect meaningful, transferable competencies — not compliance with curriculum requirements. Connect to calling and vocation explicitly.
Andragogy applied — rewriting an outcome for adult learners

Outcome violating andragogy principles: "Students will memorize the five points of Calvinism." Subject-centered, no rationale, no connection to prior experience, no practical hook.

Rewritten andragogically: "Students will apply the doctrines of grace to a specified pastoral situation — drawing on their own prior theological formation — presenting a written response that shows how each doctrine shapes their counsel."

This outcome satisfies Need to Know (why these doctrines matter pastorally), Role of Experience (prior theological formation), Problem-Orientation (a pastoral situation), and intrinsic Motivation (professional identity as a pastor-theologian).

Quality · 01

Six Common Failure Modes

These mistakes appear most consistently in course and program reviews. Each has a recognizable signature and a specific fix. Use this section when reviewing outcomes submitted by faculty.

01
Unmeasurable verb
"Students will appreciate / understand / know / become aware of…" These describe internal states. You cannot observe appreciation. You cannot assess awareness.
Fix: Replace with a Bloom's action verb. Ask: can I write a rubric criterion for this? If not, revise the verb.
02
Aspirational verb, low-level assessment
The CLO says "analyze" or "evaluate" but the only assessment is a multiple-choice exam. The outcome is honest in ambition but dishonest in evidence.
Fix: The cognitive demand of the assessment must match the verb. An Analyze CLO requires an Analyze task — not a recall quiz.
03
Too many outcomes
A course with 12 CLOs spanning recall to sermon construction is overloaded. No course can genuinely deliver on that range. The outcomes become wallpaper — present but not operative.
Fix: 3–7 focused CLOs per course. If you have more, some belong to other courses in the sequence.
04
CLO with no PLO parent
The CLO covers interesting content but cannot be traced to any PLO. It may be valuable — but it is not serving the program's stated goals. This is a structural fracture point.
Fix: Either trace it to an existing PLO explicitly, revise the PLO set, or remove the CLO and its associated work.
05
CLO ceiling exceeds PLO ceiling
The CLO asks students to Create (L6) but the PLO it serves only reaches Understand (L2). The CLO has outrun what the program promised and what the rest of the curriculum prepared for.
Fix: Check the Bloom's ceiling of the PLO. No CLO should require cognitive work the PLO does not authorize.
06
Missing condition or degree
"Students will apply hermeneutical principles." Apply them to what? Under what constraints? To what standard? Without C and D from the ABCD model, the performance is undefined.
Fix: Add condition and degree: "…to an unfamiliar Gospel narrative, in a 1,500-word exegetical brief, identifying at least three hermeneutical decisions and their rationale."
Quality · 02

From CLO to Rubric

A rubric is not a grading tool added at the end. It is the evidence that the CLO is real. If you cannot write a rubric for an outcome, the outcome is not yet written well enough to go into a syllabus.

The CLO translates directly into rubric structure. Each assessable component of a CLO becomes a criterion — a CLO with four components will typically produce four to five criteria. The Bloom's level of the CLO determines what Exemplary looks like. Exemplary must require the full cognitive demand of the CLO verb, not a lower-level approximation.

Exemplary
Demonstrates the full cognitive demand of the CLO verb — specifically, not generically. Operates at or above the target Bloom's level. Names what mastery looks like.
Proficient
Meets the CLO standard with minor gaps. Still at the target Bloom's level — just not with full depth, range, or nuance.
Developing
Falls to a lower Bloom's level. Names the specific failure mode — not "needs improvement" but exactly what cognitive step is missing and why.
Beginning
Does not meet the CLO. Names the failure clearly enough that a student knows what they produced instead of what was asked.
The Developing column is the most pedagogically valuable

Developing descriptors that name failure modes precisely — "proof-texts without exegetical support" rather than "needs improvement" — give students actionable feedback. A rubric whose Developing column only says "partially meets expectations" functions as a grading tool, not a learning tool. The specificity of the Developing descriptor is a direct test of how well the CLO was written: a vague CLO produces a vague rubric. Revising the rubric often requires revising the outcome.

The alignment self-check

Read your Exemplary descriptors. Are they operating at the Bloom's level of the CLO verb? If your CLO says "defend" (L5) but your Exemplary descriptor says "lists relevant evidence" (L1), the rubric has exposed a misalignment. The criterion language must require the same cognitive work as the outcome verb. If it doesn't, either the rubric or the outcome needs to be revised — not just one.

Quality · 03

Pre-Submission Checklist

Run every CLO through these checks before it goes into a syllabus. Use this with faculty when reviewing outcomes. Any unchecked box is a revision needed before submission.

✓
Contains a measurable action verb from Bloom's taxonomy — not "know," "understand," "appreciate," or any other unmeasurable state verb.
✓
The content domain (B) is specific enough that two instructors would independently agree on what it refers to.
✓
A condition (C) is specified — the text, situation, constraint, or audience within which the student performs.
✓
A degree (D) is specified — the quality standard, quantity, or format that defines adequate performance.
✓
I can write a rubric for this outcome — the performance is observable and gradable at the Bloom's level of the verb.
✓
This CLO can be traced to a named PLO — the connection is explicit, not assumed.
✓
The Bloom's level of this CLO is at or below the ceiling of the PLO it serves.
✓
The assignment assessing this outcome requires cognitive work at the same Bloom's level as the CLO verb.
✓
The Bloom's level is appropriate for where this course sits in the sequence — Introduce, Develop, or Master.
✓
The course has 3–7 CLOs total — not a coverage list disguised as outcomes.
✓
Every assignment in this course traces to at least one named CLO — no orphaned work.
✓
The outcome is framed in terms students will find meaningful — it connects to their vocation, not just the course requirement (Knowles' Need to Know).
Scholarship

Key References

The foundational and most-cited scholarly sources for outcome writing, Bloom's taxonomy, constructive alignment, and adult learning. Each entry notes how to use it and why it belongs in a practitioner's library.

Foundational
Tyler, R. W. (1949)
Basic Principles of Curriculum and Instruction. University of Chicago Press.
The original argument that educational objectives must precede and govern curriculum design — stated in terms of student behavior, not teacher behavior. Tyler's four questions remain the organizing logic of backward design seventy years later. Remarkably short (128 pages) and still indispensable.
↗ Internet Archive — free borrow ↗ U. of Chicago Press ↗ Open Library
Foundational
Bloom, B. S., Engelhart, M. D., Furst, E. J., Hill, W. H., & Krathwohl, D. R. (1956)
Taxonomy of Educational Objectives: The Classification of Educational Goals. Handbook I: Cognitive Domain. David McKay.
The original taxonomy. Read alongside the 2001 revision to understand what changed and why. The foundational claim — that educational objectives can be systematically classified by cognitive demand — remains the basis of all outcome writing practice. A companion affective domain taxonomy was published by Krathwohl et al. in 1964.
↗ Internet Archive — search ↗ Semantic Scholar record ↗ Krathwohl overview — TX State
Foundational
Mager, R. F. (1962)
Preparing Instructional Objectives. Fearon Publishers. (3rd ed. 1997, CEP Press.)
Established not how to classify objectives but how to write them. Mager's three-part structure (performance, conditions, criteria) became the basis of the ABCD model. His verdict on vague verbs — inadmissible — is the practical foundation of every outcome writing workshop. A short, direct, and still essential text.
↗ Mager & Bloom — practical summary ↗ Amazon — 3rd edition ↗ ABCD model in practice
Foundational
Anderson, L. W., & Krathwohl, D. R. (Eds.). (2001)
A Taxonomy for Learning, Teaching, and Assessing: A Revision of Bloom's Taxonomy of Educational Objectives. Longman.
The definitive text. Changed nouns to verbs, reordered the apex to Create, and added the Knowledge Dimension (Factual, Conceptual, Procedural, Metacognitive). All CLO and PLO Bloom's work traces here. Essential reading — not just a reference but an argument about what learning is.
↗ Semantic Scholar record ↗ Revised taxonomy overview — PDF ↗ Amazon — abridged edition
Constructive Alignment
Biggs, J., & Tang, C. (2011)
Teaching for Quality Learning at University (4th ed.). Open University Press / McGraw-Hill.
The definitive source on constructive alignment. Biggs's core argument: students learn what the assessment system signals is important, not what instructors intend. Misalignment between outcome and assessment actively misdirects learning — it does not merely produce bad grades. Practically indispensable for program and course review.
↗ Semantic Scholar record ↗ ResearchGate — preview ↗ Amazon — 4th edition
Practitioner Guide
Wiggins, G., & McTighe, J. (2005)
Understanding by Design (2nd ed.). ASCD.
The source for backward design — Stage 1 (desired results), Stage 2 (assessment evidence), Stage 3 (learning plan). The key insight: courses should be inferred from results sought, not from materials and activities with which instructors are comfortable. Design begins at the end. Operationalizes Tyler's questions into a concrete design methodology.

Practitioner Guide
Kennedy, D., Hyland, Á., & Ryan, N. (2006, revised 2009)
Writing and Using Learning Outcomes: A Practical Guide. University College Cork.
The most widely used practitioner guide for writing PLOs and CLOs across module, course, and program levels. Over 1,100 citations. Available freely online from the Cork Open Research Archive. Concrete worked examples across disciplines. Begin here for practical guidance after reading Biggs and Anderson & Krathwohl.

Complementary Framework
Fink, L. D. (2003)
Creating Significant Learning Experiences: An Integrated Approach to Designing College Courses. Jossey-Bass.
Offers a six-dimension taxonomy (Foundational Knowledge, Application, Integration, Human Dimension, Caring, Learning How to Learn) arranged not as a hierarchy but as an interactive web. Most valuable as an audit framework: after writing Bloom's-based outcomes, use Fink to check whether the Human Dimension, Caring, and Learning How to Learn are being addressed — dimensions Bloom's cognitive taxonomy does not reach.

Adult Learning
Knowles, M. S., Holton, E. F., & Swanson, R. A. (1998)
The Adult Learner (5th ed.). Gulf Publishing. (8th ed. 2015, Routledge.)
Consolidates Knowles' full andragogical framework with six core assumptions about how adults learn. Directly applicable to Westminster's student population — all adult learners with vocational callings, prior experience, and intrinsic motivation that pedagogical (child-centered) course design systematically fails to engage. Use to audit whether outcomes are framed in terms adult learners will find meaningful.
