Commonalities Among Attendees
  • Almost every student came to Reformed theology from a different tradition (Pentecostal, Baptist, charismatic, dispensational, Assemblies of God), making their WTS enrollment intentional and convictional — not incidental.
  • The majority are actively serving in local church contexts — women's ministries, youth, counseling, preaching, and eldership — making their studies immediately applicable.
  • Most students are balancing significant life demands: families, full-time careers, and ministry, which is a core reason the online program was their only viable path.
  • The workshop served as the first in-person WTS experience for most attendees, making its impact disproportionately significant.
  • A strong theme of WTS being a "lifelong dream" or deeply sought opportunity emerged across multiple profiles, indicating high personal investment in their studies.
  • Several students noted that hearing about WTS came through personal relationship — a friend, a pastor, or an alumnus — rather than advertising alone.
✦
Distinctive and Unique Voices
  • A.K. arrived as a Ukrainian immigrant whose theological transformation was catalyzed by R.C. Sproul — a story that illustrates the global reach of WTS's heritage.
  • D.J. is a retired NASA engineer whose path through nearly every major Protestant tradition over decades is one of the most distinctive in the group.
  • N.C. brings 5th-generation Christian heritage from Chinese converts in Shandong and Fujian provinces — a family faith story spanning over a century.
  • T.H. confessed faith in May 2024 — just months before the workshop — making him the most recent convert in attendance, yet he described the experience as "life-altering."
  • P.E. is an international student (British-Australian) attending across multiple homes, representing a genuinely global reach of the online program.
  • D.C. ministers specifically to military families at West Point — a highly specialized and underserved ministry context shaped by her own military family life.

Section 3

What They Said — Common Themes

Across eleven submitted reflections, four themes emerged clearly and consistently. These are not isolated comments — they were named, in different words, by the large majority of attendees.

🎓
1. Faculty Who Are Genuinely Present
  • Students were moved by professors who prayed, shared personal faith journeys, and gave generous time to students they may never meet again.
  • The informal moments — meals, off-the-cuff theological conversations, Q&A — were often named as more meaningful than formal sessions.
  • Multiple students reflected on seeing their professors as people, not merely instructors on a screen.
🤝
2. Community That Surprised Them
  • Nearly every attendee was struck by the depth of connection formed with fellow students in a short amount of time.
  • D.J. noted that friendships formed in hours felt months old. T.H. observed that a subsequent virtual small group meeting was noticeably deeper because of the campus time.
  • Shared meals, prayer, and testimonies were consistently named as formative community moments.
🏛️
3. The Physical Place Matters
  • For online students, being physically present on campus created a sense of belonging and institutional identity that is difficult to replicate online.
  • The campus tour, new building, history of WTS's founding, and chapel participation were all noted as meaningful anchors.
  • H.U. said it simply: the campus experience made her feel "like a real Westminster student."
📖
4. Theory Into Practice
  • The workshop assignment was praised for bridging academic study and actual ministry context — several students described their real-world ministry tasks being directly improved by the workshop process.
  • D.C. shared that the devotional talk she gave the following week was meaningfully stronger as a direct result of her participation.
  • S.G. highlighted the shift from theory to application as the defining value of the experience for her.

Section 5

What They'd Change

Improvement themes surfaced from submitted feedback, ranked roughly by frequency of mention. Where multiple students raised the same concern, their initials are noted.

1
Clarify the Presentation Assignment
The single most frequently mentioned improvement. Students were unclear about what was expected for the presentation — both in scope and format. Several noted that the ambiguity created unnecessary anxiety, though one student (P.M.) ultimately appreciated the flexibility it produced. Concrete examples, clearer written instructions, or a live orientation with the professor would address this directly.
A.K.D.J.H.U.J.B.N.C.T.K.
2
More Time for Presentation Feedback
The feedback session felt rushed — professors and fellow students didn't have sufficient time to offer meaningful critique to each presenter. Suggestions included splitting presentations across multiple time slots, or adding a second professor to the feedback team to share the load and extend the window.
C.T.P.M.
3
Earlier Registration and Communication
Students needed more lead time to plan travel, request time off, and prepare for the workshop. Ideally, the workshop should be listed in Course Offerings before the course begins, and registration should open earlier to allow for small group placement and logistical planning.
H.U.N.C.
4
Offer the Workshop More Frequently
Several students would like to see the workshop offered a second time each year (e.g., in the fall) and extended to additional courses. The experience was described as too valuable to remain limited to its current frequency and scope.
D.C.N.C.S.G.T.H.
5
More Structured Q&A / Discussion Time with Faculty
The panel discussion and informal faculty interactions were among the highest-rated experiences. Students want more of it — structured time for questions and extended conversation with faculty, which is difficult to replicate in online settings.
D.C.D.J.
6
Advertise the Workshop More Widely to Online Students
At least one student noted that the workshop deserves more vigorous promotion — the value is clear, and online students may not be aware of it or understand what it offers before enrolling.
A.K.
7
Logistical Small Wins
A handful of practical suggestions that would improve the onboarding experience: name tags on the first day (H.U.), a separate Canvas space for the on-campus cohort (J.B.), and a few in-classroom lecture moments once the new building is live (D.J.).
H.U.J.B.D.J.

Closing Note

Overall Assessment

  • The On-Campus Workshop produced unusually strong student satisfaction across a remarkably diverse cohort — in age, vocation, culture, theological background, and life stage. This diversity is itself a distinctive feature worth naming.
  • The most consistent finding is that in-person presence with faculty and peers generates a depth of connection and institutional identity that online delivery simply cannot replicate — and students know it.
  • Improvement feedback was constructive and actionable: the single biggest lever is clarity around the assignment. Most other suggestions are operational and addressable in near-term planning.
  • The demand signal is clear — multiple students independently asked for more frequent offerings, more courses with this format, and more time on campus. The workshop is not a nice-to-have; for this cohort, it was transformative.
